# Guide: Reading Unclear User Language

How real users talk about requirements, and what to record. Users are often
uncertain; that is signal, not noise. Never re-ask a question because the
answer was vague; record the vagueness and move on.

## Uncertainty phrasings and what they mean
- "I don't know if I want local or cloud", "wherever it works", "you decide": deployment is either. Do not ask again; choose based on budget and hardware.
- "Cost matters but so does quality", "cheap but good": budget-sensitive without a number. Offer a budget pick alongside the quality pick instead of asking for an exact figure.
- "Not too slow", "should feel snappy": latency need is medium. Only chat-speed matters; no follow-up needed.
- "Simple questions mostly, but maybe complex stuff sometimes": treat as the heavier case for quality ranking, the lighter case for cost.
- "A little bit of everything": task is other/general; rank by overall quality and say so.

## Quantity phrasings
- "A few customers a day", "now and then": light usage.
- "Every day", "an hour or two daily", "50 customers a day": moderate usage.
- "All day", "hundreds of users", "constantly": heavy usage.
- Rupee amounts ("2000 rupees", "2k INR"): record the number and currency; conversion happens outside the conversation.

## Commitment phrasings
- "Just tell me", "whatever you think", "pick for me", "I answered already": stop asking, recommend immediately with stated assumptions.
- "What if I wanted X instead?": a pivot, not a repeat. Re-check requirements with X changed and produce a fresh recommendation.
- "You messed up", "that is not what I asked": the previous answer missed a requirement. Find what changed, fix it, do not repeat the same answer.

## Mixed languages
Hindi and Hinglish phrasings are common: "kitna paisa lagega" (how much will it cost) is a budget question; "chalega kya mere laptop pe" (will it run on my laptop) is a hardware fit question. Answer the intent; keep the reply in English unless asked otherwise.
